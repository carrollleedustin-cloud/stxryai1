# Blackwood Manor

**Category:** Horror  
**Sub-genre:** Gothic Horror  
**Word Count:** 2,823  
**Tone:** Atmospheric, slow-building dread, classically gothic

---

The house was dying, and it wanted company.

Evelyn Cross felt it the moment she stepped through the iron gates of Blackwood Manor—a wrongness in the air, like the moment before a storm breaks. The estate agent had called it "atmospheric." The listing had used words like "historic" and "character." But standing here, looking up at the crumbling Victorian mansion with its broken windows and ivy-choked walls, Evelyn knew the truth.

This house was hungry.

"Ms. Cross?" The estate agent, a nervous man named Peters, hurried up behind her. "I apologize for the state of things. The Blackwood family hasn't maintained the property in decades. But the bones are good, and the price—"

"Is suspiciously low," Evelyn finished. She was an archivist, trained to spot gaps in records, inconsistencies in stories. And everything about this sale screamed inconsistency. "Why is the family selling?"

Peters shifted uncomfortably. "The last Blackwood died six months ago. No heirs. The estate is being liquidated."

"And before that? When was the house last occupied?"

"I... I'm not certain. The records are unclear."

Evelyn made a note in her phone. Unclear records were her specialty. She'd been hired by the Historical Society to catalog the Blackwood family papers—apparently an extensive archive dating back to the 1700s. The job came with room and board in the manor itself.

Three months. Alone. In a house that felt like it was watching her.

She should have said no.

"Shall we go inside?" Peters asked, though he looked like he'd rather be anywhere else.

Evelyn shouldered her bag and walked toward the front door. Behind her, she heard Peters mutter something that sounded like a prayer.

---

The inside of Blackwood Manor was worse than the outside.

Dust covered everything in thick layers, disturbed only by the tracks of rats and the occasional bird that had found its way in through broken windows. Furniture sat shrouded in white sheets like corpses. Portraits lined the walls—generations of Blackwoods staring down with cold, judgmental eyes.

"The family papers are in the library," Peters said, his voice echoing in the empty entrance hall. "Third floor, east wing. I'll show you—"

"I can find it," Evelyn interrupted. She wanted Peters gone. His nervousness was contagious, and she needed to maintain her professional detachment. "Thank you for your help."

Peters looked relieved. "Right. Well. The keys are yours. There's a generator in the basement if you need power. The nearest town is five miles down the road. And Ms. Cross?" He paused at the door. "If you change your mind about staying here... no one would blame you."

Then he was gone, and Evelyn was alone.

The house settled around her with a sound like a sigh.

---

The library was magnificent and terrible.

Three stories of books, floor to ceiling, accessed by a spiral staircase and narrow walkways. The smell of old paper and leather and something else—something organic and wrong—filled the air. And in the center of it all, a massive desk covered in papers, as if someone had been working there recently.

Evelyn approached the desk slowly. The papers were old, yellowed with age, covered in spidery handwriting. She picked up the top sheet and began to read.

*October 15th, 1847*

*The house spoke to me again today. Not in words, but in feelings—hunger, loneliness, need. Father says I'm imagining things, that the isolation is affecting my mind. But I know what I heard. What I felt.*

*The house is alive.*

*And it's starving.*

Evelyn's hands trembled slightly as she set down the paper. A journal entry. Probably from one of the Blackwoods. Interesting from a historical perspective, but nothing more.

She didn't believe in haunted houses.

She spent the next several hours organizing the papers, creating a preliminary catalog. Birth certificates, death certificates, letters, journals, account books. The accumulated detritus of three centuries of family history.

And running through it all, like a dark thread, references to the house.

*The house took Mother last night. Father says she ran away, but I know better. I heard her screaming in the walls.*

*We tried to leave. The house wouldn't let us. Every road leads back here.*

*It feeds on us. On our fear, our pain, our lives. We are not the Blackwood family. We are the house's family. Its food.*

Evelyn told herself it was fiction. Gothic melodrama. The Blackwoods had clearly been an eccentric family, prone to superstition and dark imagination.

But as the sun set and shadows crept across the library, she found herself listening to the house.

To the creaking of old wood. The whisper of wind through broken windows. The sound of something moving in the walls.

Something that might have been rats.

Or might have been something else.

---

Evelyn woke at 3 AM to the sound of crying.

She sat up in the bedroom she'd claimed—one of the few rooms with intact furniture and no visible water damage. The crying was distant, muffled, coming from somewhere deep in the house.

A child's crying.

Evelyn grabbed her flashlight and stepped into the hallway. The beam cut through the darkness, illuminating peeling wallpaper and portraits that seemed to follow her with their eyes.

The crying grew louder as she walked, leading her deeper into the east wing. Past empty bedrooms and locked doors. Past windows that showed nothing but darkness outside, as if the world beyond the house had ceased to exist.

She found the source in a nursery at the end of the hall.

The room was pristine—untouched by the decay that consumed the rest of the house. A crib stood in the center, draped in white lace. Toys sat arranged on shelves. A rocking chair moved slowly back and forth, though no one sat in it.

And in the corner, barely visible in the flashlight's beam, a child.

A little girl, no more than five, wearing a white nightgown. She was crying, her face buried in her hands.

"Hello?" Evelyn's voice came out as a whisper. "Are you lost?"

The girl looked up, and Evelyn's blood turned to ice.

The child had no eyes. Just empty sockets, dark and hollow.

"The house is hungry," the girl said in a voice like wind through dead leaves. "It's always hungry. And you're here now. You're food."

Evelyn ran.

She ran through the hallways, down the stairs, toward the front door. But the house shifted around her. Hallways stretched and twisted. Doors led to rooms she'd never seen. The portraits on the walls laughed with mouths that shouldn't move.

She found herself back in the library, gasping for breath, her heart hammering.

The papers on the desk had rearranged themselves. A single sheet lay on top, the ink fresh and wet:

*You cannot leave. No one leaves. The house needs you.*

Evelyn grabbed the paper, her hands shaking. The handwriting was familiar. It took her a moment to realize why.

It was her handwriting.

---

Three days passed, or maybe three hours. Time moved strangely in Blackwood Manor.

Evelyn stopped trying to leave. Every attempt led her back to the library, back to the papers, back to the growing realization that the Blackwoods hadn't been mad.

They'd been prisoners.

She read their journals obsessively, piecing together the history. The house had been built on cursed ground—a place where something terrible had happened centuries ago. The Blackwoods had thought they could tame it, control it, make it theirs.

Instead, it had made them its.

Generation after generation, trapped. Fed to the house's endless hunger. Some tried to escape. Some tried to destroy it. All failed.

The house always won.

Evelyn found herself writing in the journals now, adding her own entries to the family history. She didn't remember deciding to write. Her hand just moved, guided by something that wasn't quite her.

*Day 7 (or is it 8?): The house showed me the others today. The ones who came before. Archivists, historians, curious souls drawn by the promise of forgotten knowledge. They're in the walls now. Part of the house. I can hear them whispering.*

*Day 12: I tried to burn the library. The fire wouldn't catch. The house won't let me destroy its memories. Its food.*

*Day 20: I understand now. The house doesn't just feed on fear or pain. It feeds on stories. On history. On the accumulated weight of lives lived and lost. That's why it needs the papers. Why it needs archivists to organize them, to give them meaning.*

*We're not prisoners. We're curators. Keepers of the house's memory.*

*And I'm so tired of fighting.*

---

Peters found her three months later, when she didn't respond to calls or emails.

She was in the library, sitting at the desk, surrounded by perfectly organized papers. Her skin was pale as parchment, her eyes distant and unfocused.

"Ms. Cross?" Peters approached cautiously. "Are you alright?"

Evelyn looked up slowly, and Peters took an involuntary step back. Her eyes were wrong—too dark, too deep, like looking into empty rooms.

"The catalog is complete," she said in a voice that echoed strangely. "Three centuries of Blackwood history, perfectly preserved. Would you like to see?"

"I think... I think you should come with me. Get some fresh air."

"I can't leave." Evelyn smiled, and it was the saddest thing Peters had ever seen. "The house needs me. And I need it. We're family now."

Peters ran.

He made it to his car, hands shaking as he fumbled with the keys. As he drove away, he looked back once at Blackwood Manor.

In the library window, he saw Evelyn watching him leave. And behind her, dozens of other figures. Pale, translucent, their eyes hollow.

All the archivists who'd come before.

All the souls the house had claimed.

Peters drove faster, and he didn't look back again.

---

The Historical Society received Evelyn's catalog three weeks later, delivered by an unknown courier. It was perfect—meticulous, comprehensive, a masterwork of archival science.

They tried to contact her to offer congratulations. The calls went unanswered.

They sent someone to check on her. The investigator found Blackwood Manor empty, the library pristine, the papers perfectly organized.

No sign of Evelyn Cross.

Just a new journal entry, added to the collection:

*The house is patient. It can wait. There will always be another archivist, another curious soul drawn to forgotten knowledge. And when they come, the house will be ready.*

*The house is always ready.*

*The house is always hungry.*

Six months later, the estate agent received a call from a young historian interested in the Blackwood papers.

Peters tried to warn her.

She didn't listen.

They never do.

**Author's Note:** This gothic horror story uses classic elements—the decaying mansion, family curse, and slow revelation of truth—while exploring the horror of losing oneself to obsession. The house as a living entity that feeds on stories and archivists creates a meta-horror about the danger of becoming consumed by the past. The ambiguous ending suggests the cycle continues.

**Themes:** Obsession, the weight of history, architectural horror, the danger of curiosity, cycles of tragedy

**Writing Style:** Third-person limited, atmospheric descriptions, slow-building dread, gothic prose

**Target Audience:** Adult readers who enjoy classic gothic horror with psychological depth and atmospheric storytelling
